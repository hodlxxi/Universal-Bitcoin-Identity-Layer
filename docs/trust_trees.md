# Trust Trees

## Purpose
Specify Sponsorship / Recursive Accountability as the primary security and coordination mechanism in HODLXXI Trust Domains.

## Definitions
- **Trust Domain**: local coordination environment with its own policies
- **Sponsor**: participant who introduces another participant
- **Edge**: sponsorship relationship between participants
- **Branch**: subtree formed by downstream introductions
- **Trust Tree**: the full graph of Sponsor-linked Edges

## Core security rule
When a Sponsor introduces someone, the Sponsor assumes reputational accountability for that Edge and for downstream Branch behavior over time.

## Lifecycle of an Edge
1. **Introduction**: Sponsor adds a new participant into domain context.
2. **Observation**: counterparties interact and generate history.
3. **Calibration**: cooperation levels adjust to observed reliability.
4. **Escalation or contraction**: if risk rises, local actors tighten terms or prune Edges.

## Recursive accountability in practice
- Good Branch behavior improves willingness to cooperate with that Branch.
- Repeated low-integrity behavior weakens Sponsor credibility and downstream trust assumptions.
- Responses happen through local policy choices, not global coercion.

## Why tree structures matter
Trust Trees preserve causal context:
- who vouched for whom,
- where trust was earned,
- where trust degraded,
- and which relationships absorbed risk.

This context is lost when systems collapse trust into one number.

## Example pattern (guild-style)
- A Sponsor introduces two participants.
- One participant builds reliable history through repeated reciprocation.
- The other participant repeatedly violates norms.
- The Domain can narrow or remove specific Edges while preserving healthy Branches.

## Relationship to covenant and lock signals
Covenant artifacts or symbolic long-term locks can reinforce commitment intent, but they do not replace Trust Tree observation.
